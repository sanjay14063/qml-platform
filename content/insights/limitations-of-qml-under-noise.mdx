# Limitations of QML under Noise

Noise is not a secondary implementation detail in quantum machine learning—it is a defining factor of the effective model class. In many practical settings, noise determines whether learning is possible at all, regardless of circuit design.

## Where noise enters quantum systems

Quantum noise originates from multiple physical and computational sources:

- Gate errors during circuit execution
- Decoherence from interaction with the environment
- Measurement uncertainty and sampling noise (shot noise)
- Hardware-specific calibration drift and control imperfections

Each of these introduces deviations between the ideal circuit and the executed computation, accumulating across circuit depth.

## How noise affects learning dynamics

Noise directly alters optimization behavior and model expressivity:

- Gradients become noisy, biased, or vanish entirely
- Effective loss landscapes become flatter or distorted
- Signal-to-noise ratio decreases with circuit depth and system size
- Barren plateau effects are often amplified under realistic noise conditions

As a result, models that appear trainable in simulation may fail to learn on hardware.

## Simulation vs hardware gap

A persistent issue in QML research is over-reliance on noiseless or simplified simulations. In these regimes, models may show structured gradients and apparent learning capability that does not survive deployment on real quantum devices.

This gap is not cosmetic—it can completely invalidate performance claims if noise is not explicitly modeled during training and evaluation.

## Common mitigation strategies

Several approaches attempt to reduce the impact of noise, though none eliminate it fully:

- Error mitigation techniques such as zero-noise extrapolation
- Noise-aware training procedures that incorporate hardware models
- Shallow or hardware-efficient circuit designs
- Reducing circuit depth to limit error accumulation

These methods improve robustness but typically trade off against expressivity.

## Common failure mode in QML experiments

A frequent error pattern is:

1. Design and validate a quantum model in ideal simulation
2. Observe good training behavior and convergence
3. Deploy on noisy hardware
4. Experience degraded or collapsed performance

This failure mode highlights the importance of incorporating noise assumptions from the outset rather than treating them as an afterthought.

## Practical takeaway

Noise defines a hard constraint on quantum machine learning systems. Any realistic QML model must be designed and evaluated under noise conditions comparable to the target hardware.

Ignoring noise does not simplify the problem—it changes it entirely.
