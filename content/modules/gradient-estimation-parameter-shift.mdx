# Motivation

Training variational quantum circuits requires gradients $\partial C / \partial \theta_i$. Finite differences are unstable and scale poorly. The parameter-shift rule provides exact gradients for gates of the form $e^{-i\theta G/2}$ where $G^2 = I$, using only two circuit evaluations per parameter.

# Core Concepts

## Mathematical Definitions

For $U(\theta) = e^{-i\theta G/2}$ with $G^2 = I$ (e.g. $G = X, Y, Z$):

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \langle O \rangle_\theta = \frac{1}{2} \left( \langle O \rangle_{\theta + \pi/2} - \langle O \rangle_{\theta - \pi/2} \right)
$$

**General parameter-shift**: For gates with more complex spectra, similar formulas exist with additional shift terms.

## Key Equations

- **Product rule**: For chains of parameterized gates, gradients sum over each gate's contribution
- **Variance**: Each gradient component requires 2 circuit evaluations; total cost $O(2p)$ for $p$ parameters

# Failure Modes & Pitfalls

- Parameter-shift assumes perfect gates; hardware noise biases gradients
- Cost scales linearly with parameters; large circuits are expensive
- Stochastically sampled gradients (finite shots) add variance
- Some gates (e.g. CRY) require generalized parameter-shift with more terms

# Motivation

At this point, you can build a variational quantum model.

- data → feature map  
- circuit → transforms state  
- measurement → gives output  

Now comes the hard part:

> how do you *train* it?

---

In classical ML:

- gradients are computed using backprop  

In quantum ML:

- you cannot directly “differentiate through” a quantum circuit  

So the question becomes:

> how do you compute gradients when your model is a physical system?

---

# Core Concepts

## The Problem

You want:

\[
\frac{\partial C}{\partial \theta}
\]

Where:

- \( \theta \) = circuit parameter  
- \( C \) = cost function (based on measurement outcomes)  

---

Naively, you could use finite differences:

\[
\frac{C(\theta + \epsilon) - C(\theta - \epsilon)}{2\epsilon}
\]

But this is:

- noisy  
- unstable  
- inefficient  

---

## The Parameter-Shift Rule (Core Idea)

For many quantum gates:

\[
U(\theta) = e^{-i\theta G/2}
\]

Where:

- \( G \) is a generator (like \( X, Y, Z \))  
- \( G^2 = I \)  

---

Then the gradient is:

\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \langle O \rangle_\theta =
\frac{1}{2} \left(
\langle O \rangle_{\theta + \frac{\pi}{2}} -
\langle O \rangle_{\theta - \frac{\pi}{2}}
\right)
\]

---

## What This Means (important)

Instead of computing derivatives numerically:

> you evaluate the circuit twice, at shifted parameters

That’s it.

---

So:

- no approximations  
- no tiny epsilon  
- exact gradient (in theory)  

---

## Why ±π/2? (intuition)

Most parameterized gates are rotations:

\[
R(\theta) = \cos(\theta/2) I - i \sin(\theta/2) G
\]

So expectation values behave like:

- sine and cosine functions of \( \theta \)

---

And we know:

\[
\frac{d}{d\theta} \sin(\theta) = \cos(\theta)
\]

\[
\frac{d}{d\theta} \cos(\theta) = -\sin(\theta)
\]

---

The parameter-shift rule exploits this:

> shifting by \( \pm \frac{\pi}{2} \) effectively turns sine into cosine  

So the derivative becomes a difference of two shifted evaluations.

---

## Multi-Parameter Circuits

If you have:

- \( p \) parameters  

Then:

> each parameter requires 2 circuit evaluations  

Total cost:

\[
O(2p)
\]

---

## Generalized Parameter Shift

Some gates don’t satisfy \( G^2 = I \).

In those cases:

- more shift terms are needed  
- gradients become a weighted sum of multiple evaluations  

---

So:

> not all gates are equally cheap to differentiate  

---

## Intuition (3Blue1Brown style)

Think of your circuit like a machine with knobs.

Each parameter \( \theta \) is a knob.

---

To see how sensitive the output is:

- turn the knob slightly forward  
- turn it slightly backward  
- compare the outputs  

---

But instead of “slightly”:

> quantum mechanics gives you the exact shift to use  

---

# Implementation

## Automatic Gradients (PennyLane)

```python
import pennylane as qml
import numpy as np

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=2)

@qml.qnode(dev, diff_method="parameter-shift")
def circuit(params):
    qml.RY(params[0], wires=0)
    qml.RY(params[1], wires=1)
    qml.CNOT(wires=[0, 1])
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliZ(0))

params = np.array([0.1, 0.2], requires_grad=True)

grad_fn = qml.grad(circuit)
grads = grad_fn(params)
```

---

## What’s Happening Under the Hood

For each parameter:

- evaluate circuit at \( \theta + \pi/2 \)  
- evaluate at \( \theta - \pi/2 \)  
- take the difference  

---

You don’t see it, but:

> PennyLane is applying parameter-shift automatically

# Failure Modes & Pitfalls

- Using too many parameters → training becomes slow  
- Hardware noise introduces bias in gradients
