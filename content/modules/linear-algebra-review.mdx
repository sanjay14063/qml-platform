# Motivation

Quantum machine learning is, at its core, linear algebra wearing a physics costume.

If you strip away the terminology—“quantum states”, “gates”, “measurements”—what you’re really working with are:
- vectors
- matrices
- inner products

The difference from classical ML is not *what* objects you use, but **how strictly they are constrained**.

In classical ML, vectors are just containers of numbers. In quantum mechanics, vectors represent *physical states*, so they must obey rules:
- they live in complex vector spaces
- they must always be normalized
- transformations must preserve that normalization

This module is about building the mental model required to work in that world.

If you’ve seen vectors and matrices before, good. If not, that’s fine—but you’ll need to engage actively. Don’t skim this. Try to *visualize* everything.

---

# Core Concepts

## Vectors, Norms, and Geometry

Let’s start from something concrete.

A vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is something like:

$$
v = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Geometrically, this is just an arrow from the origin to the point (2, 1).

Now here’s the first shift:

> In quantum mechanics, vectors don’t live in $\mathbb{R}^n$, they live in $\mathbb{C}^n$.

That means instead of real numbers, your components can look like:

$$
v = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix}
$$

This is not just a technicality. Complex numbers allow **phase**, which is essential for interference effects later.

---

### Linear Combination (This is foundational)

Any vector can be written as a combination of basis vectors:

$$
|v\rangle = \sum_i \alpha_i |e_i\rangle
$$

For example in 2D:

$$
|v\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle
$$

Think of this as:
- $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ are directions
- $\alpha, \beta$ tell you how much of each direction you have

In quantum mechanics, these coefficients are called **amplitudes**.

Important distinction:
- amplitudes are not probabilities
- probabilities come *later*

---

### Inner Product (This is where everything connects)

The inner product is defined as:

$$
\langle u | v \rangle = \sum_i \overline{u_i} v_i
$$

This looks like a dot product, but with a conjugate.

What it actually measures:
- similarity
- alignment
- overlap

This single operation is responsible for:
- computing probabilities
- defining orthogonality
- defining length

---

### Norm (Length of a vector)

From the inner product:

$$
\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v | v \rangle}
$$

In quantum mechanics:

> Valid states must satisfy $\|v\| = 1$

This is not optional.

Why?

Because later:
- probabilities are computed as squared magnitudes
- if the norm isn’t 1, total probability ≠ 1

So normalization is enforcing:
> “this vector represents a valid probabilistic system”

---

## Orthonormal Bases

A set of vectors is orthonormal if:

$$
\langle e_i | e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}
$$

Translated:
- vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal)
- each has unit length (normalized)

Why this matters:

Any quantum state can be decomposed cleanly in such a basis.

In practice:
- computational basis: $|0\rangle, |1\rangle$
- measurement happens relative to a basis

---

## Linear Operators and Matrices

Now we move from *states* to *transformations*.

A linear operator satisfies:

$$
A(\alpha u + \beta v) = \alpha A(u) + \beta A(v)
$$

In practice, this just means:
> the transformation behaves predictably with respect to scaling and addition

These operators are represented as matrices.

---

### Key Insight (Don’t skip this)

A matrix is not just a grid of numbers.

It encodes:
> “where each basis vector gets sent”

Once you know that:
- you automatically know what happens to *every* vector

This is why matrices fully define transformations.

---

### Special Operators in Quantum Mechanics

#### Unitary Operators

$$
U^\dagger U = I
$$

Meaning:
- they preserve length
- they preserve normalization

Interpretation:
> these are the only allowed “evolutions” of quantum states

If a transformation is not unitary, it’s physically invalid (before measurement).

---

#### Hermitian Operators

$$
H = H^\dagger
$$

These represent:
> measurable quantities

Examples:
- energy
- spin
- observables in general

---

## Eigenvalues and Eigendecomposition

Now we get to one of the most important ideas.

An eigenvector satisfies:

$$
Av = \lambda v
$$

Meaning:
- direction stays the same
- only scaling happens

Interpretation:
> these are the “natural directions” of a transformation

---

### Why this matters for quantum mechanics

For Hermitian matrices:
- eigenvalues are real
- eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis

This leads to:

$$
A = \sum_i \lambda_i |v_i\rangle \langle v_i|
$$

This is not just algebra.

This is:
> the mathematical structure behind measurement

When you measure:
- the system “chooses” one eigenvector
- you observe the corresponding eigenvalue

---

## Key Equations

- Inner product: $\langle u | v \rangle = \sum_i \overline{u_i} v_i$
- Norm: $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v | v \rangle}$
- Unitary: $U^\dagger U = I$
- Eigen equation: $Av = \lambda v$
- Spectral decomposition: $A = \sum_i \lambda_i |v_i\rangle \langle v_i|$

---

## Intuition

If you compress everything:

- A **quantum state** is a unit vector
- A **quantum gate** is a unitary matrix
- A **measurement** is projection onto eigenvectors

Everything reduces to:
- vectors
- matrices
- inner products

There is no separate “quantum math”
—this *is* the math.

# Implementation

## NumPy Examples

```python
import numpy as np

# Define a normalized complex vector
v = np.array([1, 1j]) / np.sqrt(2)

# Check normalization
print("Norm:", np.vdot(v, v))

# Define a Hermitian matrix
H = np.array([[1, 1],
              [1, -1]], dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(2)

# Eigendecomposition
e_vals, e_vecs = np.linalg.eigh(H)

print("Eigenvalues:", e_vals)
print("Eigenvectors:\n", e_vecs)
