# Motivation

Quantum computers do not output deterministic values. Instead, they produce **random samples** from probability distributions. Training quantum models therefore requires statistical estimation and optimization under noise.

This module introduces probability and optimization concepts needed to understand measurement uncertainty, gradient estimation, and training dynamics in QML.

# Core Concepts

## Random Variables and Expectation

A random variable $X$ takes values according to a probability distribution $P(X=x)$.

The expectation value is:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x P(x)
$$

The variance measures uncertainty:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2
$$

## Sampling and Convergence

When estimating expectations from $N$ samples:

- The estimate converges as $N \to \infty$
- The standard error scales as $1/\sqrt{N}$

> In QML, every expectation value is estimated from *finite shots*.

## Gradients

Given a function $f(\theta)$, the gradient is:

$$
\nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta_1}, \dots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta_d} \right)
$$

Gradients indicate the direction of steepest ascent.

## Gradient Descent

To minimize $f$:

$$
\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla f(\theta)
$$

The learning rate $\eta$ controls stability and speed.

## Key Equations

- Variance scaling: $\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2 / N$
- Gradient update: $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla f$
- Noisy gradients arise from finite sampling

## Intuition

QML optimization balances:
- **Statistical noise** (shots)
- **Landscape geometry**
- **Learning rate stability**

## Gradient Descent Example

```python
import numpy as np

# Motivation

Unlike classical programs, quantum computers don’t just “give you an answer.”

You run a circuit once, you get one result. Run it again, you might get something else. What you actually get is a **sample from a probability distribution**.

That single idea changes everything.

In classical ML, you usually assume:
- your computations are exact
- gradients are clean
- outputs are deterministic

In quantum ML:
- outputs are noisy
- expectations must be *estimated*
- gradients themselves are random variables

So training a quantum model is not just optimization. It is:

> optimization under uncertainty, with limited information per step.

This module builds the intuition needed to deal with that.

---

# Core Concepts

## Random Variables and Expectation

Start simple.

A **random variable** is just something that can take different values, each with some probability.

Example:
- measuring a qubit might give 0 or 1
- but not always the same one

We write this as:

$$
P(X = x)
$$

which just means:
> the probability that $X$ takes value $x$

---

### Expectation (What you actually care about)

Instead of individual outcomes, we care about the *average behavior*:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x P(x)
$$

This is the expected value.

Interpretation:
> if you repeat the experiment many times, this is the average you converge to

In QML:
- cost functions are usually expectation values
- not single measurements

---

### Variance (How unstable things are)

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2
$$

This tells you:
> how spread out your results are

Low variance → stable  
High variance → noisy

In quantum systems, variance is unavoidable.

---

## Sampling and Convergence

Here’s the part people underestimate.

You don’t get expectations directly.

You estimate them using **samples (shots)**.

If you take $N$ samples:

- your estimate improves as $N$ increases
- but not linearly

The key scaling:

$$
\text{error} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}
$$

This is slow.

To reduce error by 10x → you need 100x more samples.

---

### Why this matters in QML

Every time you evaluate a cost function:
- you run a circuit many times
- you average the results

So:

> computation cost = circuit runs × number of shots

This is one of the biggest bottlenecks in QML.

---

## Gradients

In optimization, you care about how things change.

For a function $f(\theta)$:

$$
\nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta_1}, \dots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta_d} \right)
$$

This vector tells you:
> which direction increases the function fastest

So to minimize, you go in the opposite direction.

---

### Important shift for QML

In classical ML:
- gradients are usually exact (or very low noise)

In QML:
- gradients are **estimated from samples**
- so they are noisy

Meaning:
> even your “direction of improvement” is uncertain

---

## Gradient Descent

The basic update rule:

$$
\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla f(\theta)
$$

Where:
- $\eta$ = learning rate

---

### What actually happens in practice

Each step:
1. Estimate cost using samples
2. Estimate gradient using samples
3. Update parameters

So your update is not clean:

> it’s jittery, approximate, and sometimes wrong

---

### Learning Rate (This becomes more sensitive)

- Too large → instability, divergence
- Too small → painfully slow learning

Because gradients are noisy, choosing $\eta$ is harder than in classical ML.

---

## Key Equations

- Expectation: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x P(x)$  
- Variance: $\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$  
- Sampling error: $\sim 1/\sqrt{N}$  
- Gradient: $\nabla f$  
- Update: $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla f$

---

## Intuition

Everything in QML training is a tradeoff between:

- **Noise** (from finite sampling)
- **Cost of computation** (more shots = slower)
- **Optimization stability** (learning rate vs noise)

You are constantly balancing:

> “Do I spend more time getting a better estimate, or move faster with noisy information?”

There is no perfect answer.

# Implementation

## Gradient Descent Example

```python
import numpy as np

# Simple quadratic function
f = lambda t: t[0]**2 + t[1]**2
grad = lambda t: np.array([2*t[0], 2*t[1]])

theta = np.array([1.0, 1.0])

for _ in range(50):
    theta -= 0.1 * grad(theta)

print("Minimum:", theta)
```

---

## What to Observe

- The function is smooth → convergence is easy
- Gradients are exact → updates are stable

Now imagine:
- each gradient is slightly wrong
- each step has noise

That’s QML.

# Failure Modes & Pitfalls

- Assuming outputs are deterministic
- Using too few shots → extremely noisy training
- Using too large a learning rate with noisy gradients
- Ignoring variance in cost estimation
- Expecting smooth convergence (it often isn’t)

# Key Takeaways

1. Quantum outputs are probabilistic, not deterministic.
2. Expectations must be estimated from samples.
3. Error decreases slowly with more samples.
4. Gradients in QML are noisy.
5. Optimization is fundamentally stochastic and less stable than classical ML.
