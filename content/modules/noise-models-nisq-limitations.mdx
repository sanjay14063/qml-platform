# Motivation

So far, everything you’ve learned assumes something important:

> your quantum circuit works perfectly

But in reality:

> it doesn’t

---

Modern quantum computers are **noisy**.

Every operation you apply:

- slightly corrupts the state  
- introduces randomness  
- reduces the reliability of your output  

---

This is why the current era of quantum computing is called:

> **NISQ** — Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum  

---

Understanding noise isn’t optional.

> it determines whether your model works at all  

---

# Core Concepts

## What is Noise (intuitively)?

Noise is anything that makes your quantum state drift away from what you intended.

You prepare a state:

\[
|\psi\rangle
\]

But what you actually get is:

\[
\rho = \mathcal{E}(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|)
\]

Where:

- \( \mathcal{E} \) = noise process  
- \( \rho \) = mixed (imperfect) state  

---

So instead of a clean vector:

> you now have a *probabilistic mixture*  

---

## Why Noise is So Damaging

Quantum information is fragile because:

- it depends on precise amplitudes  
- it depends on phase relationships  
- entanglement is easily destroyed  

---

Even small errors:

- accumulate over gates  
- destroy interference patterns  
- corrupt measurements  

---

## Common Noise Models

These are simplified ways to model real hardware errors.

---

### Depolarizing Noise

\[
\mathcal{E}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + p \frac{I}{2^n}
\]

Interpretation:

- with probability \( p \), the state becomes completely random  

---

This is like:

> your system “forgets everything”  

---

### Dephasing Noise

\[
\mathcal{E}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + p Z\rho Z
\]

Interpretation:

- phase information is lost  
- amplitudes stay the same  

---

This breaks:

> interference (a core quantum effect)  

---

### Amplitude Damping

Models energy loss:

- qubits decay from \( |1\rangle \rightarrow |0\rangle \)

---

This corresponds to:

> physical relaxation of the system  

---

## Hardware Constraints

Real devices have limits:

---

### Gate Errors

Each gate has an error rate:

- typically \( 10^{-3} \) to \( 10^{-2} \)

So:

> more gates = more accumulated error  

---

### Coherence Time

- \( T_1 \): energy decay time  
- \( T_2 \): phase coherence time  

---

If your circuit runs too long:

> the system decoheres before finishing  

---

### Depth Limit

Rough idea:

\[
\text{max depth} \sim \min\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon_{\text{gate}}}, \frac{T_2}{\tau_{\text{gate}}}\right)
\]

---

So:

- deeper circuits → worse results  
- shallow circuits → more reliable  

---

## Intuition (3Blue1Brown style)

Imagine trying to:

- carefully balance a spinning object  
- while someone keeps nudging it  

---

Each gate = small nudge  

Eventually:

> the system loses its structure completely  

---

That’s noise.

---

## Why This Matters for QML

Noise directly affects:

- expectation values  
- gradients  
- training stability  

---

In practice:

- gradients become noisy  
- optimization becomes unstable  
- results vary between runs  

---

So:

> even if your theory is correct, your implementation may fail  

---

# Implementation

## Adding Noise in PennyLane

```python
import pennylane as qml

dev = qml.device("default.mixed", wires=1)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def circuit(p):
    qml.RY(0.5, wires=0)
    qml.DepolarizingChannel(p, wires=0)
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliZ(0))

for p in [0.0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2]:
    print(p, circuit(p))
```

---

## What’s Happening

- as noise increases  
- expectation values degrade  

---

Eventually:

> output becomes almost random

# Error Mitigation (brief overview)

You can try to reduce noise effects:

- run circuits at different noise levels → extrapolate  
- correct measurement errors  
- average over many runs  

---

But:

> mitigation adds cost and is not perfect  

---

Full error correction:

- requires thousands of physical qubits  
- not practical yet  

---

# Failure Modes & Pitfalls

- Trusting simulator results without noise  
- Ignoring hardware constraints  
- Using deep circuits on NISQ devices  
- Not averaging over multiple runs  
- Claiming “advantage” without noise analysis

# Key Takeaways

1. Noise turns pure states into mixed states.
2. Errors accumulate with circuit depth.
3. Coherence time limits how long circuits can run.
4. Noise degrades gradients, outputs, and training.
5. Shallow, structured circuits are more robust.
6. Error mitigation helps but does not fully solve the problem.
