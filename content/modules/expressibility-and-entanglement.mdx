# Motivation

At this point, you’ve seen how to:

- build quantum models  
- train them  
- deal with noise  

Now comes a more subtle question:

> how powerful is your circuit *actually*?

---

Not all parameterized quantum circuits are equal.

Some can represent a huge variety of quantum states.

Others are much more limited.

---

This idea is captured by:

- **expressibility** → how much your circuit can represent  
- **entanglement** → how strongly qubits are correlated  

---

But here’s the catch:

> more power does not always mean better performance  

---

In fact:

> highly expressive circuits are often harder to train  

---

# Core Concepts

## Expressibility (intuitive first)

Expressibility asks:

> how much of the quantum state space can your circuit explore?

---

You start with a circuit:

\[
|\psi(\theta)\rangle
\]

and vary parameters \( \theta \).

---

If your circuit is highly expressive:

- it can reach many different states  
- it behaves almost like a random quantum circuit  

---

If it’s not:

- it’s restricted to a small region of the Hilbert space  

---

## Formal View

We measure expressibility by comparing:

- the distribution of states your circuit produces  
- the distribution of completely random states (Haar distribution)

---

One metric uses **fidelity**:

\[
F = \mathbb{E}_{\theta,\phi}\left[|\langle \psi(\theta) | \psi(\phi) \rangle|^2\right]
\]

---

Interpretation:

- high \( F \) → states are similar → low expressibility  
- low \( F \) → states are diverse → high expressibility  

---

So:

> lower fidelity average = more expressive circuit  

---

## Entanglement

Entanglement measures how strongly qubits depend on each other.

---

For a state \( |\psi\rangle \), we split the system:

- subsystem A  
- subsystem B  

---

Then define:

\[
\rho_A = \operatorname{Tr}_B(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|)
\]

---

And compute:

\[
S(\rho_A) = -\operatorname{Tr}(\rho_A \log_2 \rho_A)
\]

---

This is **entanglement entropy**.

---

Interpretation:

- \( S = 0 \) → no entanglement  
- higher \( S \) → more entanglement  

---

## Why Entanglement Matters

Entanglement enables:

- correlations that classical systems struggle to represent  
- richer transformations of data  

---

But:

> too much entanglement can hurt training  

---

This links directly to:

- barren plateaus  
- unstable gradients  

---

## Expressibility vs Trainability

This is the key idea of the entire module:

> more expressive circuits tend to have flatter optimization landscapes  

---

Meaning:

- gradients become very small  
- optimization becomes difficult  

---

So you get a trade-off:

- high expressibility → high capacity, low trainability  
- low expressibility → easier training, limited power  

---

## Inductive Bias (very important)

Your circuit structure defines:

> what kinds of functions it prefers to learn  

---

Two common approaches:

### Hardware-Efficient Ansatz

- simple  
- matches device connectivity  
- highly expressive  

But:

- prone to barren plateaus  
- less structured  

---

### Problem-Inspired Ansatz

- designed using domain knowledge  
- more structured  

But:

- less expressive  
- often easier to train  

---

So:

> expressibility alone does not determine performance  

---

Structure matters.

---

## Intuition (3Blue1Brown style)

Imagine:

- a model that can draw *any possible shape*  
- vs a model that can draw only *specific structured patterns*  

---

The first one:

- has more power  
- but doesn’t know where to start  

---

The second:

- is limited  
- but learns faster  

---

That’s the trade-off.

---

## Connection to Earlier Topics

This module ties directly to:

- **barren plateaus** → high expressibility → vanishing gradients  
- **noise** → deeper circuits → more error  
- **VQCs** → ansatz design choices  

---

So this isn’t isolated:

> it explains why earlier problems happen

# Implementation

## Expressibility (sampling approach)

```python
import pennylane as qml
import numpy as np

n_qubits = 2
dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=n_qubits)

def ansatz(params):
    for i in range(n_qubits):
        qml.RY(params[i], wires=i)
    for i in range(n_qubits - 1):
        qml.CNOT(wires=[i, i+1])
    for i in range(n_qubits):
        qml.RZ(params[n_qubits + i], wires=i)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def get_state(params):
    ansatz(params)
    return qml.state()

def fidelity(a, b):
    return np.abs(np.vdot(a, b))**2

# sample random states and compute average fidelity
samples = 100
fid_values = []

for _ in range(samples):
    theta = np.random.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, 2*n_qubits)
    phi = np.random.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, 2*n_qubits)
    
    state1 = get_state(theta)
    state2 = get_state(phi)
    
    fid_values.append(fidelity(state1, state2))

print("Average fidelity:", np.mean(fid_values))
```

## Entanglement Entropy

```python
def entanglement_entropy(state_vector, partition):
    rho_A = qml.math.reduce_dm(state_vector, indices=partition)
    eigenvalues = np.linalg.eigvalsh(rho_A)
    eigenvalues = eigenvalues[eigenvalues > 1e-10]
    return -np.sum(eigenvalues * np.log2(eigenvalues))
```

# Failure Modes & Pitfalls

- assuming “more expressive = better model”  
- ignoring training difficulty  
- overusing deep hardware-efficient circuits  
- not considering problem structure  
- confusing randomness with usefulness

# Key Takeaways

1. Expressibility measures how much of the state space a circuit can explore.
2. Entanglement captures non-classical correlations between qubits.
3. High expressibility often leads to vanishing gradients.
4. More entanglement is not always better.
5. Ansatz design determines both performance and trainability.
6. Structure and inductive bias matter more than raw expressibility.
